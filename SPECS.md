## SECTION 1 — KNOW THE LAW

### Virginia Home Cultivation at a Glance (card grid)

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| Who can grow | Adults 21+ only |
| How many plants | **4 per household maximum** (not per person) |
| Where | Your primary residence only |
| Visibility | Cannot be seen from public view without binoculars/aircraft |
| Access | Must be inaccessible to anyone under 21 — locked area strongly recommended |
| Plant tag | Required on every plant: your name, VA ID/license number, "personal use" notation |
| Selling | **Illegal.** Any amount. |
| Concentrates | Cannot make extracts or concentrates from home-grown cannabis |
| Sharing | Can share up to 1 oz with other adults 21+ in private, without payment |
| Renters | Check your lease — landlords can prohibit cultivation |

**Practical tips for staying compliant:**
- Grow indoors in a locked room or grow tent — solves the visibility and access requirements simultaneously
- Label each plant from day one — the tag requirement applies at all growth stages
- Outdoor growers: solid privacy fence, not just hedges

**Official resource:** [Virginia Cannabis Control Authority — Home Cultivation](https://cca.virginia.gov/resources/homecultivation)

## SECTION 2 — STRAINS

### Understanding Strain Types

Cannabis is typically categorized three ways. Note: modern science shows these categories describe *plant structure* more reliably than *effects* — but they're still the most useful shorthand for new growers, especially for understanding *how to grow* each type.

---

#### Indica
- **Plant structure:** Short, bushy, 2–4 feet tall; broad dark leaves; dense compact buds
- **Flowering time:** 7–9 weeks (fast)
- **Best for:** Indoor growing (compact), beginners (manageable size)
- **Commonly associated effects:** Relaxing, body-focused, evening use
- **Grow notes:** Handles cooler temps, lower humidity; uniform growth pattern; good for small spaces

#### Sativa
- **Plant structure:** Tall, lanky, can exceed 6 feet; narrow light-green leaves; airy, elongated buds
- **Flowering time:** 10–16 weeks (slow)
- **Best for:** Outdoor growing (height room); experienced growers (longer commitment)
- **Commonly associated effects:** Uplifting, cerebral, creative, daytime use
- **Grow notes:** Needs heat and long seasons; challenging indoors without training; Virginia's warm summers suit outdoor sativas well

#### Hybrid
- **Plant structure:** Mix of indica and sativa traits; moderate height; varied bud density
- **Flowering time:** 8–10 weeks (middle ground)
- **Best for:** All skill levels; most widely available genetics
- **Commonly associated effects:** Varies; indica-dominant hybrids lean relaxing, sativa-dominant lean energizing, balanced = middle ground
- **Grow notes:** Usually the easiest to grow; most modern strains are hybrids

#### Autoflowering (Autoflower)
- **What makes it different:** Flowers automatically based on *age* (3–4 weeks), not light schedule. No need to switch lighting. Contains ruderalis genetics.
- **Plant structure:** Usually compact, 1–3 feet; fast lifecycle (seed to harvest in 60–90 days total)
- **Best for:** BEST CHOICE FOR BEGINNERS and Virginia outdoor growers. Discreet size. Multiple harvests per season.
- **Grow notes:** Very forgiving; thrives in Virginia's warm growing season; can start outdoors in late spring and harvest before fall; less training-sensitive
- **Trade-off:** Slightly lower yields and potency ceiling than photoperiod strains

#### Photoperiod vs. Autoflower — Quick Decision

| Question | Choose Photoperiod | Choose Autoflower |
|---|---|---|
| Growing indoors? | If you can control lighting | Great choice — simpler setup |
| Growing outdoors in Virginia? | Yes — follows natural seasons, harvests Oct | Yes — faster, more harvests, more discreet |
| First grow? | Possible but more to manage | **Yes — strongly recommended** |
| Want maximum yield/potency? | Yes | Not primary goal |
| Limited space? | No | **Yes** |

### Cannabinoids & Terpenes — Quick Explainer

*(Coder: expandable card or accordion panel)*

**THC (Tetrahydrocannabinol):** Primary psychoactive compound. Higher THC = stronger intoxicating effect. Most home growers target 15–25%.

**CBD (Cannabidiol):** Non-intoxicating. Associated with relaxation, anti-anxiety, anti-inflammatory effects. High-CBD strains have little to no psychoactive effect.

**CBG, CBN, CBC:** Minor cannabinoids. CBN increases with late harvest and is associated with sedative effects. CBG is the "mother cannabinoid" precursor.

**Terpenes — the real flavor and aroma drivers:**

| Terpene | Aroma | Associated With |
|---|---|---|
| Myrcene | Earthy, musky, mango | Relaxing, sedative |
| Limonene | Citrus, lemon | Uplifting, mood-boosting |
| Pinene | Pine, fresh | Alertness, memory |
| Linalool | Floral, lavender | Calming, stress relief |
| Caryophyllene | Pepper, spice | Anti-inflammatory, relaxing |
| Humulene | Hoppy, earthy | Appetite-suppressing |
| Terpinolene | Fresh, piney, floral | Uplifting, energizing |

**Entourage effect:** Cannabinoids and terpenes work together synergistically. The full chemical profile of a strain — not just THC% — shapes the actual experience.

## SECTION 3 — INDOOR GROWING

### Why Grow Indoors in Virginia?
- Full control over environment, light, temperature
- Year-round growing
- Solves legal visibility and access requirements in one setup (locked grow tent)
- Protects from Virginia's late summer humidity and pest pressure

### What You Need (Beginner Setup)

**The Core Setup:**
1. **Grow tent** (4x2 or 4x4 for up to 4 plants)
2. **LED grow light** (sized to tent)
3. **Inline fan + carbon filter** (odor control — important for privacy)
4. **Small circulation fan** (airflow)
5. **Pot/container** (3–5 gallon fabric pots recommended)
6. **Growing medium** (soil or coco coir)
7. **Nutrients**
8. **pH meter**
9. **Timer** (automates light schedule)
10. **Hygrometer/thermometer**

---

### Indoor Environment Targets

| Parameter | Seedling | Vegetative | Flowering | Late Flower |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Temperature | 72–80°F | 70–85°F | 65–80°F | 60–75°F |
| Humidity (RH) | 65–70% | 50–70% | 40–50% | 30–40% |
| Light hours | 18/6 | 18/6 | 12/12 | 12/12 |
| pH (soil) | 6.0–7.0 | 6.0–7.0 | 6.0–7.0 | 6.0–7.0 |

**Lower humidity in late flower is critical** — above 50% RH during flowering = bud rot risk.

**Temperature note:** Dropping nighttime temps 5–10°F below daytime in late flower can stimulate terpene production and bring out purple hues in some genetics.

---

### Indoor Lighting Guide

**Light schedules:**
- **Seedling → Vegetative:** 18 hours on / 6 hours off (18/6)
- **Flowering trigger:** Switch to 12 hours on / 12 hours off (12/12)
- **Autoflowers:** 18/6 throughout entire life — never need to switch

**Light spectrum:**
- Blue light (400–500nm): Vegetative growth, compact structure
- Red light (620–750nm): Flowering, bud development
- Full spectrum LEDs cover both — ideal choice

| Light Type | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Full Spectrum LED | Efficient, low heat, excellent results | Higher upfront cost | Best overall — use this |
| HPS (High Pressure Sodium) | Very powerful, proven results | Hot, higher electricity | Larger grows |
| CMH/LEC | Good spectrum, efficient | Mid-cost | Good alternative to LED |
| T5 Fluorescent | Cheap, low heat | Weak for flowering | Seedlings only |

**Recommended LED sizing:**
- 2x2 tent (1–2 plants): 200–300W actual LED
- 4x2 tent (2–4 plants): 300–400W actual LED
- 4x4 tent (4 plants max for VA): 400–600W actual LED

**Light distance (LED):**
- Seedling: 24–36" from canopy
- Vegetative: 18–24"
- Flowering: 12–18" (follow manufacturer spec)

---

### Indoor Container & Medium

**Fabric pots are strongly recommended** over plastic — roots air-prune naturally at the edges, preventing root circling, improving oxygen to roots, and dramatically improving yield.

| Container Size | Best For |
|---|---|
| 1–2 gallon | Autoflowers (final pot) |
| 3 gallon | Autoflowers or short indicas |
| 5 gallon | Standard photoperiod plants (most common) |
| 7–10 gallon | Large indeterminate or sativa strains |

**Growing Mediums:**

| Medium | pH Range | Difficulty | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-amended organic soil | 6.0–7.0 | ⭐ Easiest | Fox Farm Ocean Forest, Kind Soil — built-in nutrients; just add water early on |
| Coco coir | 5.8–6.3 | ⭐⭐ Moderate | Faster growth than soil; more frequent watering; needs full nutrients from early on |
| Hydroponics | 5.5–6.3 | ⭐⭐⭐ Advanced | See Hydroponics section |

**Best beginner choice:** Fox Farm Ocean Forest pre-amended soil. Has enough nutrients built in for the first 3–4 weeks without feeding.

---

### Ventilation & Odor Control
Cannabis has a strong odor during flowering that can carry. For privacy and courtesy to neighbors, a carbon filter + inline fan is essential for indoor grows.

**Setup:** Carbon filter → flexible ducting → inline fan → ducting out of tent (through a window or into a larger room). The carbon filter scrubs the odor before air leaves the grow space.

**Air exchange rate:** Replace the tent's air volume every 1–3 minutes. Match fan CFM to tent volume.

**Circulation fan:** Small oscillating fan inside tent, aimed at the canopy. Strengthens stems, prevents hotspots, reduces humidity buildup.

**Safety note (official Virginia CCA guidance):** Install CO2 detectors if growing indoors. Don't overload circuits or chain extension cords. High-intensity UV lights can cause eye and skin damage — turn off before working in the tent.

## SECTION 4 — OUTDOOR GROWING IN VIRGINIA

### Virginia's Climate for Cannabis

Virginia sits in USDA Hardiness Zones 5–8, with most of the state in 6–7. Summers are warm and humid — ideal for cannabis growth, but the humidity requires vigilance for bud rot and powdery mildew as harvest approaches in fall.

**Outdoor season:**
- Start seeds: Late April – Mid May (after last frost)
- Transplant outdoors: When nights stay above 50°F consistently
- Photoperiod plants begin flowering: Late July–August (as days shorten)
- Outdoor harvest window: **September–October** (before first hard frost)
- Autoflowers: Can run 2+ outdoor cycles per Virginia season

---

### Site Selection
- **Sunlight:** 6–8+ hours direct sun; south-facing is ideal
- **Privacy:** Must not be visible from public without binoculars — solid fence, solid hedge, or interior courtyard
- **Security:** Must not be accessible to anyone under 21 — locked gate or fenced area strongly recommended
- **Drainage:** Cannabis does not tolerate wet feet; avoid low spots
- **Airflow:** Good natural air circulation reduces disease pressure

---

### Soil Preparation

Outdoor cannabis grows best in rich, loamy, well-draining soil with pH 6.0–7.0.

**Soil test first.** Your local Virginia Cooperative Extension office offers free or low-cost soil tests. Tells you exactly what your soil needs.

**Amendments to work into outdoor beds:**
- Aged compost: 2–4 inches worked into top 12 inches
- Worm castings: generous handful per plant hole
- Perlite: improves drainage in clay-heavy soil
- Dolomitic lime: if pH too low (below 6.0)
- Organic slow-release fertilizer (all-purpose)

**Pre-amended raised beds** (mix of compost, soil, perlite) skip most of this work and are the recommended approach for outdoor beginners.

---

### Watering Outdoors
- Deep, infrequent: Water thoroughly, let top 2 inches dry before watering again
- In Virginia's hot summer: May need daily watering for large plants in dry stretches
- Soil test: Insert finger 2 inches into soil — dry = water, moist = wait
- Avoid overhead watering (wets buds in late flower = bud rot)
- Drip irrigation or soaker hose is ideal

---

### Mulching
Apply 2–3 inches of organic mulch around plants (straw, wood chips). Retains moisture, regulates temperature, suppresses weeds, prevents soil splash.

---

### Staking and Support
Virginia summer storms can topple large outdoor cannabis plants. Stake early with bamboo or install cages. As branches fill with heavy buds, branches may need individual support.

---

### Virginia Outdoor Grow Calendar

| Month | Task |
|---|---|
| March–April | Start seeds indoors; prepare outdoor bed/containers |
| Late April–May | Transplant seedlings after last frost (Zones 6–7: mid-May) |
| May–July | Vegetative growth; train plants; fertilize; manage pests |
| Late July–August | Plants enter flowering naturally (shorter days) |
| August–September | Flowering progresses; increase airflow; watch for bud rot |
| September–October | Harvest window; check trichomes weekly |
| Late October | Final harvest before first frost |

Same principles as tomatoes:

| Plant | Benefit |
|---|---|
| Marigolds | Deter aphids, spider mites, nematodes |

## SECTION 5 — HYDROPONICS

### Overview
Hydroponics grows cannabis in nutrient-enriched water without soil. Same fundamental rules as the tomato hydro section, but cannabis has specific pH and EC targets.

**Key advantages for cannabis:**
- Up to 50% faster growth than soil
- 90% less water than soil
- Year-round indoor production
- Eliminates soil-borne diseases

**Key challenges:**
- No buffer if pH/EC drifts (unlike soil, which has natural buffering)
- Root rot risk if oxygen/temperature not managed
- Higher upfront investment

---

### Best Systems for Home Cannabis (4 plants)

**DWC (Deep Water Culture) — Recommended**
- One plant per 5-gallon bucket
- Roots suspended in oxygenated nutrient solution
- Simple to DIY or buy as kit
- Very productive; dialed-in EC = outstanding quality
- Requires air pump, air stones, net pots

**Kratky Method (passive DWC)**
- No pump or electricity needed for water
- Plant sits above reservoir with air gap
- Good for autoflowers in smaller setups
- Monitor daily when fruiting — depletes fast

**Coco Coir with drip irrigation**
- Technically a "soilless" medium, often grouped with hydro
- Very popular — faster than soil, more forgiving than pure DWC
- Great beginner hydro bridge: familiar watering routine, hydro-speed growth
- pH target: 5.8–6.2

---

### Cannabis Hydro Numbers

**pH:**
- Target: **5.8–6.3** for hydro/coco
- Check every 2–3 days
- Most nutrient lockout problems trace to pH being off

**EC (Electrical Conductivity):**

| Stage | EC Target |
|---|---|
| Seedling | 0.4–0.8 mS/cm |
| Early vegetative | 0.8–1.3 mS/cm |
| Vegetative | 1.3–1.7 mS/cm |
| Early flower | 1.7–2.2 mS/cm |
| Peak flower | 2.2–2.8 mS/cm |
| Late flower/flush | Taper down to 0 |

**Reservoir temperature:** Keep between 65–72°F. Warm water holds less oxygen AND promotes pythium root rot. Ice bottles or a water chiller solve this.

**Change solution:** Full reservoir flush every 2 weeks. Top off with pH-adjusted water between changes.

---

### Cannabis Hydro Nutrients

**Stage 1 (Vegetative):** Higher nitrogen (N-heavy)
**Stage 2 (Flowering):** Higher phosphorus and potassium (P/K-heavy), lower nitrogen

**Recommended systems:**
- General Hydroponics Flora Trio (industry standard 3-part)
- Botanicare Pure Blend Pro (organic-based)
- Canna Coco A+B (for coco grows specifically)
- Advanced Nutrients pH Perfect line (auto-adjusts pH — easier for beginners)

**Essential supplements:**
- CalMag: Calcium-magnesium supplement — critical for cannabis in hydro and coco, especially with soft water
- Hydroguard (beneficial bacteria) — root rot prevention
- Silica supplement: Strengthens cell walls, pest and heat resistance

## SECTION 6 — GROW STAGES

*(Coder: This section works beautifully as a visual timeline — horizontal progress bar with stage markers)*

---

### Stage 1: Germination (Days 1–7)

**What's happening:** Seed shell cracks, taproot emerges, seedling pushes out of medium.

**How to germinate:**
1. *Paper towel method (easiest):* Dampen two paper towels, place seeds between them in a zip bag or plate. Keep at 70–80°F in a dark location. Check daily. Tail emerges in 1–5 days.
2. *Direct to soil:* Plant 1/4–1/2 inch deep, pointy end down, tap and tamp lightly. Keep moist.
3. *Starter cube:* Rockwool or rapid rooter plugs — best for hydro starts.

**Conditions:**
- Temperature: 72–80°F
- Humidity: 70–90%
- Light: Not yet needed (darkness for germination)
- No nutrients yet

---

### Stage 2: Seedling (Weeks 1–3)

**What's happening:** Cotyledons (first round leaves) open, followed by first set of "true" fan leaves with 1, then 3, then 5 fingers. Root system establishing.

**Care:**
- Light: 18 hours; keep lights further away to avoid burning delicate seedlings
- Humidity: 65–70% RH
- Temperature: 72–80°F
- Water: Lightly, close to stem only; seedlings are small and don't need much
- No nutrients: Pre-amended soil has enough; hydro start at 1/4 strength after first week
- Transplant caution: Handle carefully — seedlings are fragile
- Watch for: Damping off (stem collapses at soil line from fungi) = too wet; stretching = light too far

**When to move to vegetative:** 3–4 sets of true leaves, roots filling small starter container

---

### Stage 3: Vegetative (Weeks 2–8+)

**What's happening:** Rapid above-ground and below-ground growth. Plant is building the framework (stems, branches, nodes) that will support buds. The bigger and healthier the plant gets in veg, the larger the potential harvest.

**Care:**
- Light: 18/6 for photoperiod; 18/6 throughout for autoflowers
- Temperature: 70–85°F day; can drop 5–10°F at night
- Humidity: 50–70% RH
- Nutrients: **Nitrogen-heavy.** NPK roughly 3:1:2. Increase dose gradually as plant grows.
- Water: Let top 2 inches dry between waterings. Lift pot — light = dry. Feed nutrients every other watering.
- pH: Monitor 6.0–7.0 soil / 5.8–6.3 hydro

**Plant training (optional but recommended):**

| Technique | Difficulty | Benefit |
|---|---|---|
| Low Stress Training (LST) | ⭐ Easy | Tie branches down to spread canopy, more bud sites |
| Topping | ⭐⭐ Easy-Moderate | Cut main stem tip → creates 2 main colas instead of 1 |
| FIMing | ⭐⭐ Easy-Moderate | Similar to topping but less precise; creates 4 tops |
| SCROG (Screen of Green) | ⭐⭐⭐ Moderate | Horizontal screen guides canopy for even light distribution |
| SoG (Sea of Green) | ⭐⭐ Moderate | Many small plants flowered early = fast harvest |
| Defoliation | ⭐⭐ Moderate | Remove leaves blocking bud sites; improves airflow |

**Note for autoflowers:** LST only — DO NOT top or FIM autoflowers. They flower on their own schedule and don't have time to recover from high-stress techniques.

**How long to veg:**
- The longer you veg, the bigger the plant and the higher the potential yield
- Photoperiod: Flip to 12/12 when plant is at 50% of your maximum desired height (it will roughly double in size during flowering stretch)
- Autoflowers: No decision needed — they flip themselves around weeks 3–4

**Sexing your plants (photoperiod only):**
Only female plants produce buds. Males produce pollen sacks and should be removed immediately to prevent pollination (which halts bud production and fills buds with seeds).

- Female: White hair-like pistils emerge at nodes
- Male: Small round pollen sacks at nodes
- Hermaphrodite: Both (usually caused by stress — remove to prevent pollination)

**Buying feminized seeds** (see Shop section) eliminates this concern — 99%+ will be female.

---

### Stage 4: Pre-Flower (Week 7–10 for photoperiod)

**What's happening:** Transition period after 12/12 flip. Plants show sex (see above). White pistils appear at bud sites. Rapid stretch begins (plants can double in height during flowering stretch).

**Care:**
- Begin transitioning nutrients from N-heavy to P/K-heavy
- Maintain 12/12 light schedule strictly — any light leak during dark period can cause hermaphroditing
- Increase support as branches begin to fill out
- Dehumidify — begin lowering RH toward 40–50%

---

### Stage 5: Flowering (Weeks 8–16 depending on strain)

**What's happening:** Buds form and develop. Resin glands (trichomes) develop. Aroma intensifies dramatically. This is the most critical phase.

**Light:** Strict 12/12. No light leaks. Even a small light leak during dark period can stress plants.

**Temperature:** 65–80°F day, 60–70°F night. Lower end of this range in final 2 weeks promotes terpene and resin development.

**Humidity:** 
- Early flower: 45–55% RH
- Late flower: **35–45% RH** — critical. High humidity in late flower = bud rot.

**Nutrients:**
- Week 1–2 flower: Begin transitioning — reduce N, increase P and K
- Peak flower: High P/K (phosphorus for bud development, potassium for overall plant function)
- Week before harvest: Flush (plain pH-adjusted water only) — reduces nutrient residue and improves smoothness

**NPK shift summary:**
- Veg: ~3:1:2 (N-dominant)
- Early flower: ~1:3:2
- Peak flower: ~1:3:4 (P/K-dominant, low N)
- Flush: 0:0:0 (plain water)

**What to watch:**
- Buds stack and swell weekly
- White pistils (hairs) gradually darken to orange/red — watch pistil percentage
- Fan leaves begin yellowing naturally in late flower — this is NORMAL, not a deficiency
- Aroma at peak intensity 2 weeks before harvest — you can smell the plant from across the room

**Flowering time by type:**
- Fast Indicas: 7–9 weeks
- Hybrids: 8–10 weeks
- Sativas: 10–16 weeks
- Autoflowers: Count from seed, not from flip — total 65–90 days

## SECTION 7 — PEST & DISEASE ID

**Same layout concept as tomato guide: card grid, photo, danger badge, what-you-see, treatment.**

**Important rule for flowering plants:** Avoid spraying anything on buds during the last 4–6 weeks of flower. Residues on buds will be inhaled. Treat early, prevent aggressively.

---

### PESTS

---

**🔴 Spider Mites** (*Tetranychus urticae* — Two-spotted spider mite)
- **The #1 indoor cannabis pest** — by a large margin.
- **What you see:** Fine webbing on underside of leaves and between branches. Leaves develop tiny yellow/white speckles (stippling — the mite bite marks). Leaves bronze, yellow, and drop in heavy infestations.
- **ID:** Barely visible to naked eye. Shake a leaf over white paper — tiny moving specks = spider mites. Love hot, dry conditions.
- **Danger:** 🔴 HIGH — can destroy a crop in 2 weeks unchecked. Reproduce extremely fast.
- **Organic treatment:**
  - Strong water spray to undersides of all leaves (knock them off)
  - Neem oil spray (every 3 days — must hit undersides)
  - Insecticidal soap
  - Increase humidity (they slow down above 60% RH)
  - Predatory mites (*Phytoseiulus persimilis* or *Neoseiulus californicus*) — excellent biocontrol
  - Remove and dispose of heavily infested leaves
- **Prevention:** Never bring in clones or plants without quarantine; keep RH above 50% in veg; inspect plants weekly.
- **⚠️ Do not spray any miticide on flowering buds** — if caught late in flower, predatory mites are your safest option.

---

**🟠 Fungus Gnats** (*Bradysia* spp.)
- **The #1 soil pest indoors.** Adults are a nuisance; larvae damage roots.
- **What you see:** Tiny dark flies (like fruit flies but thinner) hovering around soil and lower plant. Larvae = tiny white worms in top inch of soil.
- **Damage:** Adults spread disease; larvae eat root hairs and organic matter — stunts growth, can kill seedlings.
- **Organic treatment:**
  - Let soil dry out between waterings — larvae can't survive in dry soil (most effective long-term solution)
  - Yellow sticky traps at soil level
  - Neem oil drench into soil
  - Beneficial nematodes (*Steinernema feltiae*) — drench into soil; excellent biocontrol
  - Bacillus thuringiensis var. *israelensis* (Bti) soil drench — kills larvae specifically
  - Diatomaceous earth layer on top of soil surface
- **Prevention:** Don't overwater — this is the #1 cause of fungus gnats. Don't use soil with wood chips or unaged compost.

---

**🟠 Aphids**
- Same as tomato guide. Cluster on new growth, under leaves. Produce sticky honeydew.
- **Cannabis-specific note:** Aphids can spread hop latent viroid and other viruses between plants.
- **Organic treatment:** Water blast, insecticidal soap, neem oil, release ladybugs or lacewings.

---

**🟠 Thrips**
- **What you see:** Silver or bronze streaks on leaves. Small black specks (droppings). Tiny fast-moving insects visible with magnifier — about 1mm, tan or yellow.
- **Damage:** Pierce cells and extract contents, leaving characteristic silver/bronze scarring. Spread rapidly.
- **Organic treatment:**
  - Spinosad spray (OMRI-listed, most effective)
  - Insecticidal soap
  - Neem oil
  - Blue sticky traps (attract thrips)
  - Predatory mites or *Amblyseius cucumeris* (beneficial insect targeting thrips)

---

**🟡 Whiteflies**
- Same as tomato guide. Cloud of white insects when plant disturbed.
- More common outdoors and in Virginia greenhouse grows during humid summers.
- **Organic treatment:** Yellow sticky traps, insecticidal soap, neem oil.

---

**🟡 Caterpillars / Hemp Borers**
- **What you see:** Holes in leaves, missing leaf sections, black droppings on leaves. May find tunnel damage inside buds (corn earworm/hemp borer especially in Virginia outdoor grows).
- **ID:** Various species — corn earworm (*Helicoverpa zea*) is the most destructive Virginia outdoor cannabis pest.
- **Organic treatment:**
  - Bt (*Bacillus thuringiensis var. kurstaki*) spray — OMRI-listed; harmless to humans, kills caterpillars
  - Spinosad
  - Handpick
  - Trichogramma wasp eggs (beneficial — parasitize caterpillar eggs)

---

**🟡 Broad Mites / Hemp Russet Mites** (Advanced growers — tricky)
- **What you see:** New growth is blistered, twisted, glossy, or abnormally small. Plant looks sick for no apparent reason. Often misdiagnosed as virus, pH problem, or heat stress.
- **ID:** Invisible to naked eye even under most magnifiers. Requires 60x+ microscope and experience to confirm.
- **Treatment:** Neem oil; predatory *Neoseiulus* mites; spinosad.

---

### DISEASES

---

**☠️ Bud Rot (Botrytis cinerea / Gray Mold)**
- **The most devastating disease for Virginia outdoor growers** — and a serious indoor threat.
- **What you see:** Buds that appear healthy on outside turn gray, mushy, and slimy inside. Outer leaves of affected bud turn pale then yellow/gray. White-gray fuzz visible when opened. The rot spreads rapidly to adjacent buds.
- **ID:** Starts inside dense bud tissue — easily missed until advanced. Check base of large colas. If you see one single discolored outer leaf on a large bud, look inside immediately.
- **When it strikes:** High humidity (above 50% RH) + temps 60–75°F + dense bud structure + poor airflow. Virginia's September harvest weather is prime bud rot season.
- **Treatment:**
  - **Act immediately.** Bud rot spreads via spores — any disturbance releases thousands of spores.
  - Cut affected bud cleanly, cutting 2–3cm beyond visible rot into healthy tissue.
  - Sterilize scissors between cuts (isopropyl alcohol).
  - Do not compost affected buds — bag and dispose.
  - Improve airflow immediately; lower humidity.
  - Affected buds are unsafe for consumption.
- **Prevention:**
  - Keep late-flower RH below 45% (40% ideal)
  - Good airflow through canopy — defoliate to open up dense buds
  - Avoid overhead watering in flowering
  - Outdoor: Protect from rain; shake water off plants after rain or heavy dew
  - Choose mold-resistant strains for Virginia outdoor grows (indica-dominant, airy bud structure)
  - Potassium bicarbonate spray as preventive in high-humidity conditions (not on mature buds near harvest)

---

**🔴 Powdery Mildew (PM)**
- **What you see:** White powdery or flour-like coating on leaves and stems. Starts as small circular white spots, spreads to cover entire leaf surface. Can spread to buds — **buds with PM are unsafe and should be discarded.**
- **ID:** Almost unmistakable once visible. Unlike bud rot, starts on leaf surfaces (not inside buds). Common in indoor grows with stagnant air or humidity swings.
- **Treatment:**
  - Remove affected leaves immediately
  - Potassium bicarbonate spray (1 tbsp per liter water) — changes surface pH, kills fungus
  - Milk spray (1 part milk : 9 parts water) — antifungal effect
  - Neem oil (preventive, early infection)
  - Improve airflow — this is the real fix
  - **Do not spray infected buds near harvest — discard them**
- **Prevention:**
  - Maintain RH below 55% (below 50% in flower)
  - Airflow through canopy — defoliate dense areas
  - Don't crowd plants
  - Good hygiene — sterilize tools, wash hands

---

**🔴 Root Rot (Pythium/Phytophthora)**
- **Primary hydro/coco threat.**
- **What you see:** Plant wilts even when water is adequate. Leaves yellow. Growth stalls. In DWC: roots are brown, slimy, and smell foul (healthy roots are white and smell clean).
- **Cause:** Overwatering in soil. Warm reservoir + low oxygen in hydro. Pythium thrives above 72°F water.
- **Treatment:**
  - Drain and replace reservoir with fresh, properly pH'd, oxygenated solution
  - Add Hydroguard (beneficial bacteria that competes with pythium)
  - Lower reservoir temperature below 68°F
  - Increase air stone bubbling
  - In soil: allow to dry out; improve drainage; apply beneficial bacteria
- **Prevention:**
  - Never let reservoir temp exceed 72°F
  - Run air stones 24/7 in DWC
  - Hydroguard as preventive from the start — highly recommended

---

**🟠 Damping Off**
- **Seedling killer.** Caused by Pythium and Botrytis in overly wet conditions.
- **What you see:** Healthy seedling collapses at the base of the stem overnight. Stem appears pinched, rotted, or mushy at soil line.
- **Cause:** Soil too wet, poor drainage, low airflow around seedlings.
- **Prevention:** Use well-draining seedling mix; don't overwater; gentle airflow; don't let water pool.
- **Treatment:** None — once damping off strikes a seedling, it's gone. Prevent by good practice.

---

**🟠 Nutrient Deficiency vs. Disease — How to Tell the Difference**

| Symptom Pattern | Likely Cause |
|---|---|
| Yellowing starts on LOWER, OLDER leaves | Nitrogen or mobile nutrient deficiency |
| Yellowing starts on UPPER, NEWER leaves | Calcium/iron deficiency (immobile nutrients) |
| Brown spots or patches, irregular | Calcium deficiency, or fungal disease |
| White powdery coating on leaves | Powdery mildew |
| Webbing on undersides | Spider mites |
| Blistered, twisted new growth | Broad mites, heat stress, or pH issue |
| Plant wilts despite adequate water | Root rot OR overwatering OR fusarium |
| Yellow + spots + spekling + no webbing | Thrips or fungus |

**General rule:** Check pH first. Most "mystery deficiencies" are actually nutrient lockout from pH being out of range — the nutrient is there, the plant just can't absorb it.

## SECTION 8 — HARVEST & CURE

### This Is Where It All Comes Together

The work you do in the final 2 weeks before harvest, on harvest day, and in the weeks after determines 40–50% of your final quality. Perfectly grown cannabis harvested wrong will still smoke poorly. Done right, you'll get smooth, flavorful, potent flower.

---

### Step 1: Flushing (1–2 Weeks Before Harvest)

**What it is:** Feeding only plain, pH-adjusted water for the plant's final week or two, allowing it to use up nutrients stored in its tissue.

**Why:** Removes residual nutrient "salt" buildup that can create a harsh, chemical taste when smoked. Debated in science — most experienced growers still do it. Very low downside risk.

**How long:**
- Soil: 1–2 weeks of plain water
- Coco: 1 week
- Hydro: 3–7 days (reservoir drains faster)

**Signs flush is working:** Fan leaves yellow progressively as stored nutrients deplete — this is expected and correct.

**When to start flushing:** Based on trichome color — see Step 2. Start flush when you're approximately 10–14 days from your target harvest window.

---

### Step 2: Reading Trichomes — When to Harvest

**Tools needed:** 60x jeweler's loupe ($5–15) or USB digital microscope ($20–40).

**Check trichomes on the bud (calyx) itself** — not on sugar leaves, which mature 1–2 weeks ahead and give false "ready" signals.

**The three stages:**

| Trichome Color | What It Means | Harvest? |
|---|---|---|
| Clear / Transparent | Immature; THC not fully developed | Too early — wait |
| Cloudy / Milky White | Peak THC content | Harvest window for max potency |
| Amber | THC converting to CBN; more sedative effect | Slightly past peak; relaxing/body-heavy effect |

**Target windows:**

| Goal | Trichome Ratio |
|---|---|
| Maximum THC potency | 70–90% cloudy, 5–10% amber, minimal clear |
| Balanced effect | 50–70% cloudy, 20–30% amber |
| Relaxing/sedative | 70%+ amber |

**Secondary harvest signals (use to confirm, not as primary):**
- **Pistils:** 70–90% of white hairs have darkened to orange/red/brown
- **Calyx swelling:** Buds look plump, stacked, firm to gentle touch
- **Fan leaf yellowing:** Natural senescence in late flower
- **Aroma:** Smell is at absolute peak intensity
- **Trichome coverage:** Buds visually dripping in frost

**Harvesting too early** is the #1 beginner mistake. The last 1–2 weeks add 15–25% of final weight and peak trichome development. Be patient.

---

### Optional: 48-Hour Darkness Pre-Harvest

Some growers place plants in complete darkness for 48 hours before harvest. Theory: the plant reacts to perceived end-of-season and pushes final terpene/resin production. Scientific evidence is limited but many experienced growers swear by it. Low risk. Do it or don't — your call.

---

### Step 3: Harvest Day

**Timing:** Morning, after the plant's nighttime dark period — terpene and cannabinoid levels are thought to peak after dark.

**Tools:** Sharp, clean scissors or pruning shears (sterilize with isopropyl alcohol). Latex/nitrile gloves (resin is sticky and contains cannabinoids — your hands will be covered). Trimming tray or bowl.

**Cut method:**
1. Cut main stalk or individual branches — whichever is manageable for your space
2. Remove large fan leaves immediately (minimal trichomes, make drying uneven)
3. Keep sugar leaves for now if wet trimming later; or remove now if wet trimming

**Wet trim vs. Dry trim:**
- **Wet trim:** Remove sugar leaves now while plant is fresh. Faster, easier to trim. Shorter dry time. Better for humid climates (Virginia) — less moisture trapped in buds.
- **Dry trim:** Remove sugar leaves after drying. Slightly longer dry time. Some argue better terpene preservation. Works best in very dry climates.

**For Virginia's humidity:** Wet trimming is generally recommended.

---

### Step 4: Drying

**This stage is non-negotiable.** Rushing the dry ruins months of work.

**Drying environment:**
- Temperature: **60–70°F** (65°F ideal)
- Humidity: **55–62% RH**
- Airflow: Gentle — fan on low to circulate air, NOT directly blowing on buds
- Darkness: Dark room — light degrades THC
- No heat sources nearby

**How to hang/set up:**
- Hang individual branches upside-down on a line or hanger
- Or use mesh drying racks (gentle, even drying)
- Give each bud space to breathe — no crowding

**How long:** 7–14 days. Depends on bud density, temperature, and humidity.

**Done when:** Small stems snap cleanly when bent. Larger stems still slightly flexible is fine. Outer bud is dry to touch, not bone-dry like crumbles to dust.

**Too fast:** Buds that smell like hay/grass during drying are drying too fast — temp too high, humidity too low. Slow it down.

**Too slow:** Risk of mold if humidity stays above 65% — increase airflow gently.

**Stem snap test:** Bend a small interior stem between fingers. Snaps = ready to cure. Bends/folds = needs more time.

---

### Step 5: Curing

**This is what separates good flower from exceptional flower.** Curing breaks down chlorophyll (the "green" harsh taste), allows remaining moisture to redistribute evenly, and lets terpenes develop their full aromatic complexity.

**Minimum cure: 2 weeks. Ideal cure: 4–8 weeks. Exceptional cure: 3–6 months.**

**What you need:**
- Airtight glass mason jars (wide-mouth)
- Digital hygrometer (fits inside jar)
- Boveda or Integra Boost humidity packs (58–62% RH two-way packs — highly recommended)

**The curing process:**

1. Fill jars 3/4 full (don't pack) — buds need airspace
2. Seal jars and store in cool, dark location
3. **Burping:** Open jars daily for the first 2 weeks — 10–15 minutes each time. Releases excess moisture, allows fresh air exchange. If you smell ammonia when opening, there's too much moisture — leave jars open longer and check for mold.
4. After 2 weeks: Burp every 2–3 days
5. After 4 weeks: Weekly burping is enough
6. With Boveda/Integra packs: The two-way pack maintains target RH automatically — more forgiving if you miss a burp

**Target RH in jar:** 55–62%
- Above 65%: Mold risk — open immediately and dry further
- Below 50%: Trichomes desiccate and degrade — introduce humidity pack

**How you know it's working:** Smell transforms from "green/grassy" to rich, complex, strain-specific aroma. Buds feel slightly spongy but firm. Smooth smoke or vapor.

**Long-term storage:** After 6+ week cure, store sealed jars with humidity pack in a cool dark place. Well-cured flower stored properly maintains quality for 1–2 years.

## APPENDIX B — CANNABIS GLOSSARY

- **Photoperiod:** Strain that flowers based on light schedule (12/12)
- **Autoflower:** Strain that flowers based on age, not light
- **Feminized:** Seeds bred to produce only female plants (99%+ female)
- **Trichomes:** Tiny resin glands on buds; contain THC, CBD, terpenes
- **Calyx:** The small structure that forms each bud — where trichomes concentrate
- **Cola:** Main bud cluster at top of plant or branch
- **Node:** Point on stem where branches emerge
- **Sucker/Lateral shoot:** Side branch growing from node
- **Topping:** Cutting main stem tip to create two main colas
- **LST (Low Stress Training):** Tying branches down to spread canopy
- **SCROG:** Screen of Green — horizontal net to train canopy
- **Veg (Vegetative stage):** Growth phase before flowering
- **Flip:** Switching lights from 18/6 to 12/12 to trigger flowering
- **Flush:** Feeding only plain water before harvest
- **Terpenes:** Aromatic compounds; determine smell, flavor, and influence effects
- **EC:** Electrical Conductivity — measures nutrient concentration
- **PPM:** Parts per million — alternate measure of nutrient concentration
- **RH:** Relative Humidity
- **IPM:** Integrated Pest Management — prevention-first, targeted treatment approach
- **Wet trim:** Trimming sugar leaves immediately after harvest
- **Dry trim:** Trimming after drying is complete
- **Burping:** Opening curing jars briefly to exchange air
- **Pistils:** The hairs on buds; change from white to orange/red at maturity
- **Brix:** Sugar content of plant tissue — higher = more developed
